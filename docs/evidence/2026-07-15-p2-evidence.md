## Dual-narrowband anchor search

Reviewed sources:

- [Cloudy Nights 760211](https://www.cloudynights.com/forums/topic/760211-d1-vs-l-extreme/):
  same f/3.7 OTA and camera, same 10-minute California/Heart integrations, B4 with an
  IDAS D1 versus B8 with an L-eXtreme. The author judged the B8 dual-band views better,
  but later described it as a casual, uncontrolled comparison; the mount/site/filter all
  differ and no calibrated SNR or equal-quality time ratio is supplied.
- [Cloudy Nights 832729](https://www.cloudynights.com/forums/topic/832729-beginner-dual-narrowband-filter-question/):
  provides B8 examples with f/4.8, measured SQM 18.4–18.7, and named dual/tri-band
  filters, but the cross-sky equal-time statement is explicitly approximate rather than a
  controlled result.
- [Cloudy Nights 969663](https://www.cloudynights.com/forums/topic/969663-best-broadband-filters-for-bortle-7-8-skies/):
  estimates SNR gains from filter transmission curves, including a dual 3 nm example, but
  it is a theoretical spectral calculation rather than a same-target, same-system,
  two-sky integration outcome.
- [Brill dual-band filter assessment](https://cdn.astrobin.com/ckeditor-files/78130/2025/62de555d-1c16-4372-98d9-e4bed24566be.pdf):
  supplies measured passbands, angle-of-incidence behavior, and controlled filter SNR
  comparisons, but not the sky-brightness versus total-integration ratio required by the
  current coupling model.

Conclusion: these sources establish that dual narrowband can preserve strong emission
contrast under bright skies, but none yields a defensible equal-quality time ratio across
measured sky brightness with controlled target and optics. `dual_nb=0.30` therefore
remains explicitly unanchored and unchanged.
